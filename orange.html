Orange

Simplify store managers' daily routine

Context
I was part of the design team that helped Orange simplifying the daily routine of their store managers. We designed an edible view of all the financial data they have to follow in order to manage their boutique performance.
Before our arrival, store managers used to use Excel files because¬†the internal tool that was available to them had a complex navigation and displayed inconsistency in the data provided.
As the portal didn't fit their needs, store managers began to build their own tool through Excel files; given just as many excel files as store managers (~ 4000).
To a company level, this finally led to a poor customers knowledge and increase the employees' frustration for 2 reasons: 1) store managers wasted several hours to maintain and update these files. 2) the time spent on these files was taken from the time that managers should have spend with their sales teams.
Understanding store managers' way of working
We started the project with¬†a use analysis by attending several store managers'¬†meetings.
What emerged was a need to steer boutiques in two different way:
¬† ¬† ¬†‚Ä¢ ¬†a daily use with a focus on the boutique's sales performance in real time
¬† ¬† ¬†‚Ä¢ ¬†a use with a focus on decision making based on results ¬†
A store manager briefing his team directly on outlook
Meeting store managers in their work context allowed us to understand our design stakes. We had to design a tool that helps visualising¬†performance over time, for different kind of users and with the possibility to make comparaison between data.
Co-creation
We kick-started our design phase with a workshop with store managers and project committees in order to understand their data and to assess them in terms of purpose, targeted-user and frequency.¬†
By a series of different exercices, we managed to map the ecosystem of all the data needed
Design
Building the foundation
The content is presented in the form of views. The user is free to create and to organise the views he needs. For each view, the user can also select a hierarchical level, a period of time and add the KPIs needed.
Sketching the core content
The KPIs are organised on a grid by using a system of cards. According to the information density, a card can have four different sizes. Each KPI is graphically displayed according to its type and its level of information necessary to the user.
Merging
The designed core content was then embedded into the overall structure.
Iteration and user testing
The interface was then tested at various stages of development with different level of users. Evaluations were made either remotely or directly with users. We iterated based on the feedback we had during these sessions.
UI principle: the glanceability
To reduce the time spent on the dashboard, we wanted to maximise¬†the ability to understand¬†information¬†in¬†a glance. To build this glanceability, each card can be composed by 3 different graphical layers. In terms of colours, we decided to highlight in red very low values, in yellow the "not bad" values and in green the optimal values.
Final implementation
The reference card was build in the form of a square of 240px. This allowed to position a maximum of 4 squares in the useful content area of the interface and gave rhythm on each view. ¬†Regarding the colours, we decided to use them only in the details of the cards in order to not overwhelmed the interface and the users.¬†
The left card displays an insufficient value with a critical trend compare to the last period.
The right card displays a normal situation.
